AFR_1497
New laws needed for driverless motoring;Guest opinion

TOTAL WORDS: 628
The classic "trolley car" moral conundrum harks back to the 1960s. But it's never been more relevant.What would you do if a runaway trolley was on a path to kill five people tied to railway tracks? Would you intervene by pulling a lever to divert the trolley, instead killing one person on a side track not previously at risk?With the advent of autonomous vehicles this is less of a thought experiment and more of a reality. Truly driverless cars require a fundamental rethink not just about safety concerns, but also about cars and how we use them more broadly. We need to "let go of the steering wheel". There will always be a place for enthusiasts (just as people still own horses), but truly driverless cars cannot have a steering wheel.Earlier this month Paul Retter, chief executive and commissioner of the National Transport Commission (NTC), released an important discussion paper which identifies 716 driver-, road- and vehicle-related provisions in our state, territory and federal laws requiring some degree of revision before autonomous vehicles can take to our roads.He's called for a review ahead of any widespread launch of driverless vehicles.A consideration of existing liability regimes, such as who is responsible if your driverless car stalls in the middle of a freeway, causing a pile-up, is required. But a more fundamental rethink must also be undertaken, including new technology questions: do we need laws for dangerous driving if the underlying software was properly certified?What security standards will be mandated to reduce the chance of cars being hacked? The internet of everything with its associated privacy questions also remains an issue. Will your travelling metadata be made available without a warrant?International regulators are grappling with these issues. However, we still frequently see the notion that a sane, accountable driver must retain some overriding ability to control a driverless car.Germany is already calling for an international code to be developed to define a series of product safety standards for autonomous vehicles and for the auto industry to encrypt autonomous vehicle data and communications. Australia must play its part and be prepared. There needs to be a framework around the use of hardware and software in autonomous vehicles, mandated delivery of updates and patches to software (in a secure way that cannot be hacked), meaningful service-level agreements, constructed not only for safe telecommunications networks but also roadside assistance and servicing, with clear guidelines established on the right of access to data, both private and anonymised.In a world where studies show that 90 per cent of accidents are caused by human error and where the cost of a driver is the largest component of your taxi or Uber car, driverless cars are inevitable.The NTC's discussion paper on driverless vehicles raises important issues, and the commission's plan is to take its recommendations to the nation's transport ministers by November this year, signalling the pace of change. Although some issues will be simpler to resolve than others, a root and branch rethink is still required.To return to the trolley problem and to ensure a lively discussion over dinner with friends, you should think about some of the logical and ethical decisions that driverless cars present.What should a driverless car do when headed for an out-of-control school bus? Does it maintain its position - likely killing everyone in the car and injuring children or veer left into an elderly couple not otherwise in danger?These ethical questions are important issues - if at the outer edge - and the sooner we all start talking about them, the better we can start to see the answers and really let go of the steering wheel.

The proposed legislation addresses these needs.Communications data retention policies exist in one form or another in many countries throughout the world, including liberal-democracies in North America and Europe (and the UK). They are more pervasive in non-democratic states such as China and Russia and throughout the Middle East and Africa, where there is little public debate and heinous violations of freedoms that Western communities take for granted.US reforms mirror Australian approachUS President Barack Obama, who was a civil rights attorney and taught constitutional law at the University of Chicago, declared in a January speech: "I believe it is important that the capability that [the US metadata] program is designed to meet is preserved."In defending intelligence agencies' "valuable" and "necessary" bulk metadata collection, which had "generated the most controversy", Obama explained that after a review by top civil libertarians and constitutional lawyers, which "turned up no indication [the] database has been intentionally abused", he was implementing reforms that mirror the Australian approach.Instead of government collecting bulk metadata, private companies will be required to retain the information, which can then be queried on a targeted individual basis.There is a vital democratic debate Australians should continue to have to ensure surveillance powers are only used when necessary and are subject to unfettered oversight by Parliament and its proxies.Currently, the oversight regime includes, but is not limited to, the Inspector-General of intelligence and security, the National Security Committee of Cabinet, the parliamentary joint committee on intelligence and security, the Australian Commission for Law Enforcement Integrity, the Commonwealth Law Enforcement Ombudsman and the Independent National Security Legislation Monitor.If there is a persuasive argument for more constraints on, and monitoring of, agencies without unacceptable costs to the security of our liberties, the majority of Australians would doubtless support it.

Meet CBA's new breed of tech-savvy execs.Around this time last year, Commonwealth Bank of Australia staff members received a $250 gift card from chief executive Ian Narev.
